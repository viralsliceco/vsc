TikTok's Creator Health Rating quietly became the most important number in a creator's business this spring. One score now determines whether you stay in brand campaigns, keep Creator Rewards payouts flowing, and hold your TikTok Shop access — and thousands of accounts found out the hard way when it started moving without warning.

The **TikTok Creator Health Rating** (CHR) is a live score TikTok uses to measure how well a creator's account and content comply with its policies. It sits behind every monetization decision on the platform in 2026: Creator Rewards Program eligibility, TikTok Shop campaign participation, and brand deal access all run through this single gating mechanism. If your score drops, the dollars stop.

## The TikTok Creator Health Rating Is Now an Active Gatekeeper

For most of TikTok's history, policy compliance was reactive. You posted, something got flagged, you took it down. The Creator Health Rating changes that model entirely.

Since spring 2026, CHR operates as a live, continuous score attached to every monetized account. [TikTok's DFYD 2026 campaign documentation confirms](https://ppc.land/tiktok-shop-dfyd-2026-uses-live-scoring-to-remove-creators-mid-campaign/) that removal from active brand campaigns can happen to any creator — Affiliate or Merchant-Type — the moment their CHR falls to 150 or below. Not at the end of a campaign. Mid-flight.

That's the shift. CHR isn't a report card you check once a quarter. It's a live dial that TikTok turns — and if it hits the floor while you're in the middle of a paid promotion, you're out.

This matters because [TikTok monetization in 2026](https://www.socialcal.app/blog/how-to-monetize-tiktok) routes through more channels than ever — Creator Rewards, Shop affiliate, brand campaigns, Series, and subscription features — and every single channel now checks CHR before paying out. Lose the score, lose access to all of them simultaneously.

## How the CHR Score Is Actually Calculated

TikTok hasn't published a precise formula, but the inputs are clear from [policy documentation and Seller University updates](https://seller-us.tiktok.com/university/essay?knowledge_id=7096334743553838&lang=en).

The score aggregates compliance signals across three dimensions: content violations, account behavior, and commercial activity. Content violations — community guideline strikes, advertising policy flags, prohibited product claims in Shop — carry the heaviest weight. Account behavior includes things like spam signals and authentication issues. Commercial activity flags include misleading claims in affiliate posts or promoting restricted product categories.

Each violation subtracts points. Sustained clean behavior over time adds them back. The score floor that triggers campaign removal sits at 150, per the DFYD campaign guide. The ceiling and the exact point values per violation type aren't public, but the practical target based on creator reports is staying above 200 to operate with a safety buffer.

New creators aren't exempt. [TikTok Seller University](https://seller-us.tiktok.com/university/essay?knowledge_id=7096334743553838&lang=en) confirmed a pathway that lets early-stage creators begin monetizing on Shop before hitting traditional follower thresholds — but their CHR is tracked from day one. Violations during that probationary period can crater a score before the creator even qualifies for full access.

## The 90-Day Decay Rule That Blindsided Thousands of Accounts

The CHR 90-day decay rule is the mechanism that caused the most damage in May and June 2026 — and it's the one most creators didn't see coming.

Here's how it works: TikTok doesn't just score your future content. It retroactively re-reviews content you posted up to 90 days ago against its *current* standards. [The retroactive sweep confirmed in spring 2026](https://ppc.land/tiktok-shops-tightening-grip-new-rules-that-could-freeze-your-account/) included bulk re-review of previously approved content against updated guidelines. Posts that passed moderation in February could fail in May — and that failure hits your CHR today.

This is why so many creators woke up to lower scores without having posted anything new or violating. Their history moved underneath them.

### Why the 90-day window is the number to know

Ninety days is long enough to include a meaningful chunk of most creators' active content libraries. If you post daily, that's 90 posts under potential re-review. If your niche sits near any policy gray area — supplements, financial products, certain beauty claims — the retroactive risk is highest.

[TikTok's own Discover documentation on the CHR decay rule](https://www.tiktok.com/discover/why-is-my-tiktok-promotion-taking-so-long-to-review) frames this as a feature, not a bug: the platform wants content compliance to reflect current standards, not the rules that were in place when a video was first posted. From a creator's perspective, it means your 90-day content window is an open liability until you audit it.

## What the Enforcement Wave Actually Looked Like on the Ground

The spring 2026 enforcement shift wasn't subtle. Creators in TikTok Shop affiliate programs reported being removed from campaigns they were actively running. Some saw CHR drops of 40–60 points in a single week with no new posts.

The pattern was consistent: accounts in high-scrutiny product categories — health supplements, skincare with efficacy claims, financial services — took the hardest hits. Posts that had been approved under 2025 guidelines were being re-flagged under stricter 2026 advertising standards, specifically around claim language and disclosure requirements.

[The enforcement posture TikTok adopted in spring 2026](https://ppc.land/tiktok-shops-tightening-grip-new-rules-that-could-freeze-your-account/) included not just CHR scoring but parallel action on seller accounts — frozen payouts, suspended Shop access, and account-level flags that compounded the score damage. Creators running both Shop affiliate and their own merchant accounts faced double exposure.

The accounts that fared best had two things in common: they'd already been running quarterly content audits, and they'd kept claim language conservative enough to survive a tighter reading of the guidelines. The accounts that got hit hardest were optimizing for conversion copy without building in any policy buffer.

## The Contrarian Read: CHR Is a Competitive Moat If You Run It Right

Most takes on the Creator Health Rating frame it as a threat. That's the wrong frame.

TikTok's live scoring system removes low-compliance creators from campaigns mid-flight. That means every brand campaign that goes live has a self-selecting filter: only creators who've maintained their CHR above the threshold stay in the pool. If you're above 200 while competitors are scrambling at 160, you don't just survive the cut — you absorb their campaign placements.

[TikTok monetization in 2026](https://www.socialcal.app/blog/how-to-monetize-tiktok) is concentrating toward creators who treat compliance as infrastructure, not as a reactive problem to manage when something breaks. The CHR system rewards exactly that operating posture.

The same dynamic applies to the Creator Rewards Program. As the CHR floor filters out more accounts, the remaining eligible pool gets more of the distribution budget. A 10% reduction in eligible creators doesn't mean 10% more revenue for the survivors — it means campaign budgets that would have gone to removed creators need somewhere to go.

This is a moment to build a structural advantage, not just avoid a penalty.

## Six Moves to Protect Your TikTok CHR Score This Week

These are specific, shippable actions — not general advice.

**1. Run a 90-day content audit right now.** Pull every post from the last 90 days and cross-reference it against TikTok's current [Community Guidelines](https://www.tiktok.com/community-guidelines) and [TikTok Shop policies](https://seller-us.tiktok.com/university/essay?knowledge_id=7096334743553838&lang=en). Flag anything with efficacy claims, before/after framing, or prohibited product categories. Delete or edit before the retroactive sweep hits it.

**2. Strip claim language to what you can prove.** "This supplement changed my life" is a flag. "I've been using this for 30 days" is not. Reframe affiliate content around personal experience and observable behavior rather than outcome claims. This is the single highest-impact edit for accounts in health, beauty, and finance niches.

**3. Set a weekly CHR check as a recurring task.** Because the score is live, weekly monitoring is the minimum cadence. A 20-point drop is recoverable. A 60-point drop that you caught three weeks late is a crisis. Build the check into your Monday workflow.

**4. Build a 50-point buffer above any threshold that matters to you.** If 150 is the campaign removal floor, your operating target is 200. If your Creator Rewards access requires 100, run at 150. Margin absorbs the decay rule's surprise drops.

**5. Check your product category exposure in TikTok Shop.** Some categories carry structural CHR risk regardless of how clean your content language is. If you're promoting products in regulated categories, verify the products themselves are currently approved — [Shop enforcement in 2026](https://ppc.land/tiktok-shops-tightening-grip-new-rules-that-could-freeze-your-account/) has been hitting product-level flags that cascade into creator account scores.

**6. Document your compliance decisions.** If you're making a judgment call on a piece of content — deciding a claim is acceptable — write it down with the guideline version you checked against. If TikTok's standards update and you get retroactively flagged, that documentation informs how you appeal.

## What to Watch Over the Next 90 Days

The signal that confirms this enforcement posture is permanent: watch whether TikTok extends the retroactive review window beyond 90 days. If they push it to 180, the platform is signaling that historical compliance is now a permanent liability, not a rolling window. That would require a fundamentally different content archiving strategy.

Watch also for CHR score transparency improvements. Right now, the score exists but granular breakdown — which violations cost how many points — isn't surfaced in the Creator Center. If TikTok publishes a detailed score breakdown (similar to how Seller ratings work on other platforms), it would shift the compliance game from defensive auditing to targeted repair. That's a product change worth tracking.

Finally, watch brand campaign briefs. As agencies and brands get smarter about CHR, expect to see minimum CHR thresholds written directly into influencer contracts — not just as a TikTok-side gate, but as a contractual requirement from the brand side. That would make your TikTok account health score a negotiating variable in every deal you sign.

## The Takeaway

The TikTok Creator Health Rating is no longer a background signal — it's the active filter between you and every monetization dollar on the platform. The 90-day decay rule means your compliance problem isn't just today's content; it's everything you've posted in the last three months, re-reviewed against rules that may have just changed. Run the audit now, build the buffer, and treat CHR maintenance as infrastructure rather than damage control. The creators who do that this week will be the ones still in campaigns when the next enforcement wave hits.
