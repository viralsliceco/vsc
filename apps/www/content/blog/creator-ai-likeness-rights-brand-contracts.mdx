Brands are rewriting creator contracts right now, and the change isn't in the payment terms. It's buried four pages deep, in a clause about 'likeness and derivative works' that looks almost identical to the one you signed last year — except this version hands over your face, voice, and image to train AI models, permanently, for free.

Creator AI likeness rights are the new battleground in brand deals. [Forbes reported on May 15, 2026](https://www.forbes.com/sites/ianshepherd/2026/05/15/the-creator-economys-next-war-is-over-ai-ownership/) that standard boilerplate likeness clauses have quietly mutated into perpetual AI training rights — and most creators don't catch it until the campaign is over and the model is already built.

## The Clause That Changed Without a Memo

For years, a likeness clause in a brand deal meant one thing: the brand can use your face and name in ads for the duration of the campaign. Reasonable. Understood. Signed.

In 2026, that same section is doing something very different. Brands are adding language that grants rights to use your likeness to create 'AI-generated or synthetic content,' to 'train machine learning models,' and to do so on a 'perpetual, irrevocable, royalty-free basis.' That last phrase — perpetual and irrevocable — is the problem.

Once you sign it, the brand owns a version of your face and voice that outlives every campaign, every relationship, and potentially your entire career.

[Inc. documented this pattern](https://www.inc.com/jasmine-browley/creators-data-could-be-worth-millions-and-theyre-giving-it-away-for-free/91339569) with a sharp observation: creators are building on infrastructure they don't control, and their likeness, data, and audience relationships are increasingly claimed by platforms and brands as a byproduct of doing business.

## Why This Is Happening Now

Brands need training data, and creator content is some of the richest available. Your on-camera delivery, your vocal cadence, your gesture patterns — all of it is exactly what generative AI models need to produce synthetic influencers that perform like you, at zero marginal cost per post.

The math is obvious. A one-time $10,000 sponsored post that also grants perpetual AI training rights is a dramatically better deal for a brand than a $10,000 post that doesn't. So the clauses are getting added. Quietly. In legal language that doesn't announce itself.

[Digiday's state-of-the-industry piece](https://digiday.com/media/the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-creator-economy/) framed the bigger shift: creators who win in this era won't just have the most views — they'll have the best systems and the tightest control over their owned assets. Creator data ownership is infrastructure now, not an afterthought.

The stakes got visceral this week when Dua Lipa's $15 million likeness lawsuit against Samsung started circulating on LinkedIn. That's not a cautionary tale from a theoretical future. It's active litigation, right now, over exactly this category of rights.

## What a Dangerous Contract Looks Like

You don't need a law degree to spot the red flags. When you get a contract, run a keyword search before you read anything else.

Search for: **likeness, synthetic, generated, AI, machine learning, train, perpetual, irrevocable, derivative works.** Any sentence combining 'likeness' with 'perpetual' or 'irrevocable' deserves a full stop and a direct question to the brand's team.

Specifically, watch for these three structures:

**Broad IP assignment** — language that assigns all 'content, likeness, and derivatives' to the brand. This can sweep in AI-generated versions of you without naming them.

**Sublicensing rights** — if the brand can sublicense your likeness to third parties, an AI vendor they work with can train on you without ever contacting you.

**No expiry on AI usage** — campaign rights that expire in 90 days but AI training rights that don't expire at all. Both can exist in the same contract.

[New influencer contract disclosure requirements taking effect in 2026](https://www.valueyournetwork.com/en/influencer-law-2026-contracts/) are meant to force brands to be explicit about AI usage. But those rules apply to new agreements — they don't retroactively fix what you've already signed.

## The Terms You Should Be Demanding

Creators who are ahead of this are doing three things. The Forbes piece names them specifically.

**Time-limited AI licenses.** Any AI usage rights should expire — ideally at the same time as the campaign itself, or at most 12 months out. 'Perpetual' should be a non-starter.

**Approval rights over AI-generated output.** If a brand creates synthetic content using your likeness, you should have the right to review and reject it before it goes live. This is standard in music licensing; it needs to be standard here.

**Kill switch clauses.** This is the emerging norm that creator-side attorneys are now pushing for. A kill switch is a contractual provision that lets you revoke AI usage rights — [Forbes specifically surfaced this](https://www.forbes.com/sites/ianshepherd/2026/05/15/the-creator-economys-next-war-is-over-ai-ownership/) as a concrete, actionable demand. If a brand refuses to include one, that tells you exactly how they plan to use what you're signing over.

Some creators are also pushing for revenue participation clauses tied to AI usage volume. If a brand runs 10,000 impressions of a synthetic version of you, you should see a portion of that value. This is harder to negotiate but not unreasonable — and the Dua Lipa case is going to make brands more receptive to it over the next 12 months.

[RSL Media launched on May 12, 2026](https://markets.businessinsider.com/news/stocks/rsl-media-launches-centering-human-consent-in-the-ai-era-1036143938) specifically to address the consent gap in AI-era creator agreements, building a media company around the principle that every person should control how their work, identity, and likeness is used by AI. The fact that a company exists to solve this problem is a signal of how widespread the problem actually is.

## How to Audit Your Current Agreements

If you've signed brand deals in the last 18 months without specifically looking for AI language, pull those contracts today. You can't undo what you signed, but you can know what's out there — and you can factor it into renewal decisions.

For any new deal, add one sentence to your standard counter-terms: *'Notwithstanding any other provision, Brand shall not use Creator's likeness, voice, or any content derived therefrom to train, fine-tune, or develop any artificial intelligence or machine learning system without Creator's separate written consent and agreed compensation.'*

That sentence, or something like it reviewed by your own attorney, is the floor. [Internal reference: see our breakdown of creator contract structures on our creators page](/creators).

Brands will push back. Some will walk. The ones who walk were planning to use the clause.

## The Takeaway

Creator AI likeness rights are not a future problem — they're in contracts being signed this week. The brands building AI training pipelines on creator data are doing it with language that's been in deals for years, updated just enough to cover synthetic output. Audit every new contract for the words 'perpetual,' 'AI,' and 'synthetic' before you sign anything. Demand a kill switch. If the brand won't include one, the clause is doing exactly what you think it is.
